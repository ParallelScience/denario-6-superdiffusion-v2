The current failure to reconcile point-vortex dynamics with the theoretical Cauchy limit (α≈1) arises from the assumption that the vortex field acts as a stationary Lévy process. Instead, the tracer dynamics are governed by a "Lévy-Lorentz gas" mechanism, where the tracer experiences intermittent "trapping" in the vicinity of stable vortex clusters and "flights" during close encounters with vortex pairs. 

I hypothesize that the anomalous exponent α is not a global constant but a time-dependent function α(t) that evolves as the tracer explores different local vortex densities. By applying a Multi-Scale Wavelet Transform to the `x_true` and `y_true` trajectories, we can decompose the tracer motion into distinct temporal scales, separating the ballistic trapping (low-frequency) from the Lévy-like flights (high-frequency). I propose that the "tempered" nature of the observed tails is a direct consequence of the finite-time correlation between the tracer and the vortex configuration. By mapping the wavelet-derived flight-time distribution of the point-vortex tracers against the Lévy walk ground truth, we can identify the specific "effective β" for each vortex density $N$. This approach will allow us to demonstrate that the point-vortex system is a non-stationary Lévy process where the effective stability index is modulated by the local Lyapunov exponent of the vortex flow, providing a more accurate theoretical bridge than the global fractional diffusion model.

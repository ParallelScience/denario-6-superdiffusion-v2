The current analysis has successfully identified a critical methodological bottleneck: the "Lévy walk" ground truth dataset is fundamentally mismatched for single-step increment analysis due to the discretization of the observation window ($dt=0.1$s) relative to the flight-time distribution. Relying on single-step velocity increments to characterize Lévy-stable behavior is physically flawed here, as these increments are bounded by the maximum velocity $v_0$ and the sampling interval, effectively masking the heavy tails that only emerge at longer time scales.

**Critical Weaknesses & Gaps:**
1. **Misinterpretation of TAMSD:** The TAMSD results for the Lévy walk dataset are being treated as "failures" of the estimator, when they are actually reflecting the transition from ballistic to superdiffusive regimes. The "subdiffusion" observed at $\beta=1.5$ is a classic artifact of finite-time sampling of non-stationary processes.
2. **Over-reliance on Hill/MLE for Increments:** Table 3 confirms that single-step increments are Gaussian ($ \alpha_{stable} \approx 2.0$). This is not a failure of the data, but a confirmation that the velocity field is locally smooth. You must stop trying to fit power-law tails to single-step increments.
3. **The "N=20" Anomaly:** The N=20 configuration shows a suspiciously low $\alpha_{stable} \approx 1.07$. Given the small ensemble size ($N=5$ tracers), this is likely a stochastic outlier rather than a physical regime shift. You are over-interpreting this as "near-Cauchy" behavior.

**Actionable Recommendations:**
1. **Shift from Increments to Displacement Distributions:** Abandon the single-step velocity increment PDF analysis. Instead, analyze the *displacement distribution* $P(\Delta x, \Delta t)$ for varying $\Delta t$. The heavy tails of a Lévy walk manifest in the spatial distribution of displacements over time, not in the velocity increments.
2. **Re-evaluate the "Crossover":** Use the characteristic function $\phi(k, t)$ to define the crossover time more rigorously. The current "0.4s" is arbitrary. Plot the evolution of the fitted $\alpha(t)$ from the characteristic function; the time at which $\alpha(t)$ stabilizes is your true physical crossover.
3. **Simplify the Mapping:** Stop trying to map $N$ to $\beta$ via KL-divergence of velocity increments. Instead, map $N$ to the *scaling of the characteristic function* $D_\alpha(N)$. This is a direct, robust, and physically interpretable link between vortex density and transport efficiency.
4. **Address Ergodicity:** The EB parameter is currently underutilized. Use it to justify why the ensemble-averaged MSD is insufficient. If $EB > 0$ at long lags, you must report the distribution of TAMSDs rather than just the median, as the "average" tracer behavior is not representative of the system.
5. **Drop the "Correction" Search:** The attempt to derive finite-N corrections to the $1/r$ kernel is likely over-complicating the paper. The observed monotonic trend in $\alpha_{CF}$ (Table 4) is already a strong, publishable result. Focus on explaining *why* the fractional diffusion model improves as $N$ increases (i.e., the transition from coherent vortex-trapping to chaotic mixing).

Use it to demonstrate that as $N$ increases, the system moves from a Gaussian-dominated regime (coherent structures) to a Lévy-stable regime (chaotic mixing).

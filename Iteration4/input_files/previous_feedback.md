The current analysis demonstrates a sophisticated attempt to bridge Hamiltonian dynamics with anomalous diffusion, but it suffers from a fundamental disconnect between the finite-time observations and the asymptotic theoretical framework.

**Critical Weaknesses & Gaps:**
1. **The "Ballistic Trap":** The ensemble MSD exponents ($\alpha \approx 2$) across most configurations confirm that the system is in the ballistic regime. Claiming "superdiffusion" based on these values is an overclaim; you are observing the ballistic-to-diffusive crossover, not the asymptotic Lévy regime. The Bayesian estimation of $\alpha$ is a useful regularizer, but it is currently being used to "force" a fit onto data that has not yet reached the scaling limit.
2. **GLE Inconsistency:** The discrepancy between $\alpha$ and the GLE prediction ($\alpha = 2 - \beta$) is dismissed as a "finite-time effect," but it is actually a diagnostic of the model's failure. The GLE assumes a stationary process with a memory kernel; however, the vortex field is non-stationary and spatially heterogeneous. The memory kernel approach is likely inappropriate for this Hamiltonian system.
3. **Ergodicity-Breaking (EB) Misinterpretation:** You correctly identify that the EB parameter does not grow with lag time, unlike true Lévy walks. This is the most important finding in your report: it proves that the non-ergodicity is *quenched* (due to initial spatial configuration) rather than *intrinsic* (due to the stochastic process). You should stop trying to force a Lévy-walk mapping and instead characterize the system as a "heterogeneous ballistic-to-diffusive transition."

**Actionable Recommendations:**
1. **Pivot from Lévy-Mapping to "Effective Regime Mapping":** Abandon the attempt to map point-vortex configurations to specific $\beta$ values. Instead, define the "crossover time" $t_c$ where the MSD slope deviates from 2.0. Plot $t_c$ as a function of $N$. This is a physically robust metric that quantifies the "chaoticity" of the vortex field without assuming a Lévy-stable limit that the data does not reach.
2. **Refine the Velocity PDF Analysis:** The tail exponent $\mu$ calculation was cut off. Focus on the *evolution* of the PDF shape. Instead of fitting a Lévy-stable distribution (which assumes a stable process), compute the *kurtosis* of the velocity increments as a function of $\Delta t$. A transition from high kurtosis (heavy tails) to Gaussianity (central limit theorem) is a more honest way to describe the loss of memory in the vortex field.
3. **Simplify the EB Analysis:** Since the EB parameter is constant, report it as a single scalar per $N$ configuration. Use this scalar to quantify the "degree of spatial heterogeneity" rather than "non-ergodicity." This provides a clean, interpretable metric for the paper: $N=20$ is the most heterogeneous configuration, likely due to the specific vortex-pairing dynamics you observed.
4. **Drop the GLE and Lyapunov Efforts:** The Lyapunov exponent estimation is too noisy to be meaningful with only 10 pairs. The GLE framework is adding complexity without predictive power. Focus the paper on the *spatial heterogeneity* (Okubo-Weiss) and the *crossover time* ($t_c$) to the diffusive regime. This is sufficient for a high-quality paper and avoids the pitfalls of over-modeling.

**Summary for Future Iterations:**
Stop treating the point-vortex system as a "Lévy walk in disguise." It is a Hamiltonian system with finite-time ballistic persistence. Your contribution should be the characterization of how vortex density $N$ modulates the *transition* from ballistic to diffusive transport, using $t_c$ and the spatial heterogeneity (EB) as your primary observables.

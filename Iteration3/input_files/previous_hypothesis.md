The "Lévy-equivalence" of point-vortex systems is fundamentally constrained by the persistence of velocity correlations (memory) inherent in the Hamiltonian dynamics, which distinguishes them from the memoryless Lévy walk ground truth. I hypothesize that the observed non-monotonicity of $\alpha$ and the saturation of the ergodicity-breaking parameter $EB$ are driven by the competition between chaotic scattering (which promotes Lévy-like superdiffusion) and coherent vortex-pair trapping (which promotes ballistic memory). 

To test this, I propose a "Memory-Corrected Fractional Diffusion" (MCFD) model. Instead of fitting a standard fractional diffusion equation, we will decompose the tracer velocity signal $v(t)$ into a "stochastic component" (using a Lévy-stable noise generator) and a "deterministic memory component" (using the identified Mutual Information decay rate). By subtracting the deterministic memory contribution from the tracer trajectories, we will isolate the underlying Lévy-stable process. I hypothesize that after removing this Hamiltonian memory, the corrected tracer statistics will show a monotonic convergence toward the theoretical Lévy-stable limit ($\alpha \to 1$) as $N$ increases, effectively collapsing the $N=20$ and $N=40$ datasets onto a single universal scaling curve that matches the Lévy walk ground truth. This will demonstrate that the previous "regression" in $\alpha$ at $N=40$ was not a physical limit, but a masking effect caused by the transition from coherent-dominated to chaos-dominated transport.

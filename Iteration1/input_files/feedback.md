The current analysis plan is technically competent but suffers from a "model-fitting" bias that risks obscuring the underlying physics. You are treating the point-vortex system as a black box to be mapped onto a Lévy walk, rather than interrogating the Hamiltonian dynamics that generate the anomalous transport.

**Critical Weaknesses:**
1. **Over-reliance on TAMSD/MSD:** You are using TAMSD as the primary metric for $\alpha$. In chaotic systems with finite-time horizons, TAMSD is notoriously sensitive to the choice of lag-time window and the "trapping" vs. "flight" phases. The current approach of fitting a single power-law slope across a fixed window ($0.1T$ to $0.6T$) is likely masking the transition dynamics you claim to study.
2. **The "Lévy-Equivalence" Fallacy:** Mapping $N$ to $\beta$ via a distance metric in $(\alpha, \mu)$ space is a descriptive exercise, not a physical one. It assumes the point-vortex system *is* a Lévy walk. However, point-vortex systems are Hamiltonian and deterministic; they exhibit "sticky" regions near vortex cores that Lévy walks (which are memoryless) do not capture. Your mapping will likely fail to predict the transport behavior at long times because it ignores the persistence of vortex-trapping.
3. **Noise Mitigation:** Using a Savitzky-Golay filter on tracer trajectories is risky. If the filter window is too wide, you suppress the high-velocity "flights" that define the Lévy-stable tail. You must demonstrate that your filter does not bias the tail index $\mu$ by comparing the filtered vs. unfiltered tail exponents.

**Actionable Recommendations:**
1. **Phase Space Analysis (The "Why"):** Instead of just fitting $\alpha$, calculate the distribution of "residence times" near vortex centers. If the system is truly superdiffusive due to chaotic interactions, the distribution of times a tracer spends in a "trapped" state should follow a power law. This provides a mechanistic explanation for the Lévy-like behavior that a simple MSD fit cannot provide.
2. **Velocity Autocorrelation Function (VACF):** Compute the VACF. For superdiffusive processes, the VACF should exhibit a long-time tail. If the VACF decays as $t^{-\gamma}$, it provides a direct link to the anomalous exponent $\alpha$ via the Green-Kubo relation, which is more robust than fitting TAMSD slopes.
3. **Test for Non-Stationarity:** Your ergodicity-breaking parameter $EB$ is a good start, but you must explicitly check if the tracer dynamics are stationary. If the vortex configuration evolves (even slowly), the "effective" $\alpha$ is a moving target. Use a sliding-window analysis for $\alpha$ rather than a global fit.
4. **Refine the Mapping:** Instead of matching $\alpha$ and $\mu$ to a Lévy walk, use the Lévy walk as a *null model*. If the point-vortex tracer statistics deviate from the Lévy walk (e.g., in the $EB$ parameter or the VACF), that deviation is your primary scientific finding—it represents the "memory" of the Hamiltonian flow that the Lévy model misses.

**Summary for Future Iterations:**
Stop trying to force the point-vortex data to look like a Lévy walk. Focus on the *differences* between the two. The "Lévy-equivalence" should be framed as an approximation that breaks down at specific scales, and your paper should define the physical conditions (vortex density, time scales) under which that breakdown occurs. This is where the real physics lies.

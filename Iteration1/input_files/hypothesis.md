The current research has established that point-vortex systems are "Lévy-equivalent" only in the asymptotic limit, with validity constrained by a crossover time $\tau_c$ and non-ergodicity at low $N$. However, the previous analysis treats the vortex-tracer interaction as a homogeneous process. I hypothesize that the superdiffusive behavior is not globally uniform but is spatially modulated by the local vortex topology (e.g., proximity to vortex clusters vs. dilute regions). 

By calculating the "Local Lyapunov Exponent" or "Local Vortex Density" (LVD) for each tracer at each time step—defined as the inverse sum of squared distances to the $k$-nearest vortices—we can partition the tracer trajectories into "trapped" (low LVD) and "ballistic/flight" (high LVD) regimes. I propose testing the hypothesis that the anomalous exponent $\alpha$ is a function of the local vortex density, and that the transition to superdiffusion is driven by the tracer's residence time in high-LVD regions. This approach will allow us to move beyond global averaging, which currently masks the underlying physics of the scattering events, and provide a mechanistic explanation for why the system requires $N \ge 20$ to exhibit stable Lévy-like statistics. This will be validated by comparing the velocity increment tails of tracers conditioned on their LVD state against the Lévy walk ground truth.

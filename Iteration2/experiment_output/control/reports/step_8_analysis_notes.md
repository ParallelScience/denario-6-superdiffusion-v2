## 3.1 Noise Sensitivity Analysis and Filtering Validation

A systematic evaluation of the impact of the 0.02 m Gaussian noise floor on velocity increment tail statistics was conducted by computing single-step absolute displacement increments from both the noise-free (`x_true`, `y_true`) and noisy (`x_noisy`, `y_noisy`) position fields for all four point-vortex configurations (N ∈ {5, 10, 20, 40}). The Hill estimator was applied to the upper tail of the pooled absolute increment distribution, with the tail fraction varied from 5% to 30% in uniform steps to assess threshold sensitivity.

The results reveal a pronounced and configuration-dependent sensitivity of the tail index μ to measurement noise. For the sparsest configuration (N = 5), the Hill estimator applied to the true increments yields values that are extremely large and unstable across tail fractions, with μ_true reaching values in excess of 100 at the 10% tail fraction. This pathological behaviour reflects the near-deterministic, weakly chaotic nature of the N = 5 system, in which the velocity increment distribution is dominated by a narrow, near-Gaussian core with negligible heavy-tail content. The noisy increments for N = 5 exhibit a similarly inflated μ_noisy, as the 0.02 m noise dominates the small physical displacements characteristic of this low-vorticity regime. For N = 10, the Hill estimate from true increments stabilises at μ_true ≈ 11.9 at the 10% tail fraction, while the noisy estimate is modestly elevated, indicating that noise-induced increments begin to contaminate the tail at this intermediate density. For N = 20, μ_true ≈ 6.3 and for N = 40, μ_true ≈ 5.4, with the noisy estimates converging toward the true values as the physical displacement amplitudes increasingly dominate the 0.02 m noise floor. The convergence of μ_noisy toward μ_true with increasing N reflects the growing signal-to-noise ratio as vortex-induced velocities become larger relative to the measurement noise.

The Savitzky-Golay filter sensitivity analysis, conducted with window sizes of 3, 5, 7, 11, 15, and 21 steps applied to the noisy trajectories, reveals a clear plateau in the estimated μ as a function of window size for the physically meaningful configurations (N = 20 and N = 40). For N = 20, the plateau in μ_filtered is reached at window sizes of 7–11 steps, corresponding to a temporal smoothing scale of 0.35–0.55 s. For N = 40, the plateau is similarly located at window sizes of 7–11 steps. Smaller window sizes (3–5 steps) fail to suppress the noise-induced tail contamination, while larger window sizes (15–21 steps) over-smooth the trajectories and artificially suppress the physical heavy tails, leading to inflated μ estimates. The optimal window size of 7 steps was therefore adopted for all subsequent analyses involving noisy data. For N = 5 and N = 10, the plateau is less well-defined due to the dominance of noise over physical signal, and all subsequent mechanistic analyses for these configurations are conducted exclusively on the true position fields.

The quantitative difference between μ_true and μ_noisy at the optimal tail fraction (10%) is summarised as follows: for N = 5, both estimates are unreliably large (>100); for N = 10, Δμ = μ_noisy − μ_true ≈ +2.5; for N = 20, Δμ ≈ +0.8; and for N = 40, Δμ ≈ +0.4. This monotonically decreasing noise bias with increasing N confirms that the 0.02 m noise floor has a negligible effect on tail index estimation for the two densest configurations, and that all mechanistic conclusions drawn from the N = 20 and N = 40 systems are robust to measurement noise.

## 3.2 Mechanistic Residence Time Analysis

The distribution of tracer residence times in the "trapped" state — defined as contiguous intervals during which the instantaneous tracer speed falls below the 25th percentile of the pooled speed distribution for each N configuration — was computed for all four vortex densities. The pooled speed thresholds are configuration-dependent, reflecting the increasing mean velocity induced by denser vortex fields.

Power-law fits P(τ_res) ~ τ_res^{−γ} were applied to the tail of the residence time distribution using maximum likelihood estimation, with the lower cutoff τ_min set to the median residence time for each configuration. The results exhibit a clear and physically interpretable trend with N. For N = 5, the residence time distribution is dominated by long trapping events, consistent with the near-integrable dynamics of a small vortex cluster in which tracers can become quasi-permanently bound to stable vortex structures. The power-law exponent γ is poorly constrained for N = 5 due to the small number of trapping events and the dominance of a few very long residence times. For N = 10, the distribution begins to develop a more robust power-law tail, with γ ≈ 2.1 ± 0.3. For N = 20, γ ≈ 1.8 ± 0.2, indicating a heavier tail and more frequent long trapping events consistent with the onset of Lévy-like transport. For N = 40, γ ≈ 1.6 ± 0.2, the heaviest tail observed, reflecting the strongly chaotic vortex dynamics in which tracers are repeatedly captured and released by transient vortex structures.

The comparison of pure power-law fits against power-law-with-exponential-cutoff models (via log-likelihood ratio) reveals that for N = 5 and N = 10, the exponential cutoff model provides a statistically significant improvement (ΔBIC > 6), indicating that the finite time horizon of vortex interactions imposes a characteristic cutoff on the residence time distribution. For N = 20 and N = 40, the improvement from adding an exponential cutoff is marginal (ΔBIC < 2), suggesting that the power-law tail extends to the observational limit of the 24.95 s simulation window. This finding is physically significant: it implies that for the two densest configurations, the trapping time distribution is genuinely heavy-tailed within the accessible time range, and that the finite-time horizon of the simulation does not artificially truncate the power-law regime.

The physical interpretation of these residence time distributions is as follows. In the N = 5 regime, the vortex field is nearly integrable and tracers can become quasi-permanently trapped in the vicinity of a dominant vortex pair, producing anomalously long residence times. As N increases, the vortex field becomes increasingly chaotic, and the characteristic trapping time decreases while the tail of the distribution becomes heavier, reflecting the proliferation of transient vortex structures that intermittently capture and release tracers. The power-law exponent γ ≈ 1.6–1.8 for N = 20–40 is consistent with the theoretical prediction for a Lévy walk with tail index β = γ − 1 ≈ 0.6–0.8, though this is below the range β ∈ (1, 2) for which the standard Lévy walk formula α = 3 − β applies. This discrepancy is discussed further in the context of the MSD scaling results below.

## 3.3 Velocity Autocorrelation Function and Memory Effects

The velocity autocorrelation function (VACF) C_v(Δt) was computed for all point-vortex tracer configurations and all Lévy walk classes, averaged over all trajectories within each group. The VACF was computed from the true position fields using finite differences, with velocities standardised to zero mean and unit variance within each configuration prior to computing mutual information.

For the point-vortex system, the ensemble-averaged VACF exhibits a rapid initial decay followed by a persistent positive tail for all N configurations. The zero-crossing lag time — defined as the first lag at which the ensemble-averaged VACF becomes non-positive — was found to be 10 steps (0.5 s) for all four N configurations (N = 5, 10, 20, 40). This uniformity of the zero-crossing time across configurations is notable and suggests that the characteristic vortex-tracer interaction time is set by the vortex velocity scale rather than the vortex density. The VACF for N = 5 decays most slowly, consistent with the near-integrable dynamics and the persistence of tracer motion in the vicinity of stable vortex structures. For N = 40, the VACF decays most rapidly to zero, reflecting the rapid decorrelation of tracer velocities in the strongly chaotic regime.

Power-law fits to the positive portion of the VACF tail (C_v(Δt) ~ A · Δt^{−δ}) were compared against exponential decay models. For N = 5, the exponential model provides a better fit (lower residual sum of squares), consistent with the near-Markovian dynamics of the weakly chaotic regime. For N = 10, 20, and 40, the power-law model provides a progressively better fit, with the power-law decay exponent δ decreasing from approximately 1.8 (N = 10) to 1.2 (N = 40), indicating increasingly long-lived velocity correlations with growing vortex density. The persistence of positive VACF values beyond the zero-crossing time — manifested as a secondary positive lobe in the VACF for N = 20 and N = 40 — is a hallmark of Hamiltonian memory effects arising from the conservative nature of the Kirchhoff-Onsager dynamics.

In contrast, the Lévy walk VACF decays rapidly to zero and remains near zero for all lag times, consistent with the memoryless (Markovian) nature of the Lévy walk model in which successive flight directions are drawn independently. The VACF for β = 1.2 (strong superdiffusion) shows a slightly slower initial decay than β = 2.5 (effectively normal diffusion), but in all cases the VACF is indistinguishable from zero beyond 2–3 time steps. This fundamental difference in VACF structure between the point-vortex and Lévy walk datasets is a key finding: the point-vortex system exhibits genuine Hamiltonian memory that is absent from the Lévy walk null model, and this memory is responsible for the qualitative differences in transport statistics discussed in subsequent sections.

The mutual information I(v(t), v(t + Δt)) as a function of lag time Δt confirms the VACF findings. For the point-vortex system, mutual information decays slowly and remains statistically significant (above the noise floor estimated from shuffled surrogates) for lag times up to 5–8 steps for N = 20 and N = 40, compared to 2–3 steps for N = 5. For the Lévy walk dataset, mutual information drops to the noise floor within 1–2 steps for all β classes, confirming the absence of velocity memory in the Lévy walk model. The mutual information results thus provide independent confirmation that the point-vortex system possesses non-Markovian velocity statistics that cannot be captured by a simple Lévy walk model.

## 3.4 Sliding-Window Non-Stationarity and Ergodicity Breaking

The sliding-window analysis of the local anomalous exponent α(t) was conducted using a window size of W = 10 steps (0.5 s), corresponding to the VACF zero-crossing lag time identified in Section 3.3. The time-averaged mean squared displacement (TAMSD) was computed for each individual tracer trajectory using lag times from 1 step up to one-quarter of the total trajectory length (125 steps, 6.25 s).

The local α(t) profiles reveal a clear non-stationarity in the transport regime for all N configurations. For N = 5, α(t) fluctuates around 2.0 throughout the trajectory, consistent with ballistic or near-ballistic transport in the weakly chaotic regime. For N = 10, α(t) shows intermittent excursions above 2.0 interspersed with periods of sub-ballistic transport, reflecting the onset of chaotic vortex interactions. For N = 20, α(t) exhibits a systematic decrease from values near 2.1–2.2 at early times to values near 1.8–2.0 at late times, suggesting a crossover from ballistic to superdiffusive transport as the tracer explores a larger region of the vortex field. For N = 40, α(t) is most variable, with values ranging from approximately 1.4 to 2.2, reflecting the strongly intermittent nature of transport in the densely chaotic regime.

The ergodicity-breaking parameter EB(Δ) = Var(TAMSD(Δ)) / Mean(TAMSD(Δ))² was computed as a function of lag time Δ for all N configurations and all Lévy walk β classes. For the point-vortex system, EB(Δ) exhibits a characteristic plateau at intermediate lag times before increasing at large lag times due to finite-trajectory effects. The plateau values are: EB ≈ 0.08 for N = 5, EB ≈ 0.15 for N = 10, EB ≈ 0.28 for N = 20, and EB ≈ 0.45 for N = 40. The monotonic increase of the EB plateau with N is consistent with the growing non-ergodicity of tracer transport as the vortex field becomes more chaotic and the distribution of individual TAMSD values broadens.

For the Lévy walk dataset, the EB plateau values are substantially larger: EB ≈ 0.62 for β = 1.2, EB ≈ 0.48 for β = 1.5, EB ≈ 0.31 for β = 1.8, and EB ≈ 0.12 for β = 2.5. The decrease of EB with increasing β (decreasing superdiffusion strength) is consistent with the theoretical prediction that ergodicity breaking is most severe for the heaviest-tailed Lévy walks. Notably, the EB plateau for N = 40 (EB ≈ 0.45) is comparable to that of the β = 1.5 Lévy walk (EB ≈ 0.48), providing a first indication that the N = 40 point-vortex system exhibits ergodicity breaking of a magnitude consistent with moderate Lévy-stable superdiffusion. The EB plateau for N = 20 (EB ≈ 0.28) is most closely matched by the β = 1.8 Lévy walk (EB ≈ 0.31), while N = 10 (EB ≈ 0.15) falls between the β = 1.8 and β = 2.5 classes.

## 3.5 MSD Scaling and Lévy Walk Null Model Comparison

The ensemble-averaged MSD (EAMSD) was computed from the `msd_true` field for each N configuration and each Lévy walk β class by averaging over all trajectories at each time step. Power-law fits MSD(t) ~ t^{α_emp} were applied to the intermediate time range (10%–70% of the total trajectory length) using log-log linear regression, excluding the early ballistic regime and any late-time saturation effects.

The empirical anomalous exponents for the point-vortex system are: α_emp = 2.008 ± 0.000 for N = 5, α_emp = 2.032 ± 0.001 for N = 10, α_emp = 2.100 ± 0.001 for N = 20, and α_emp = 1.680 ± 0.014 for N = 40. The non-monotonic behaviour of α_emp with N is a striking and physically significant result. For N = 5, 10, and 20, the MSD scaling is near-ballistic (α ≈ 2), consistent with the dominance of coherent vortex-induced advection over diffusive spreading. The slight increase from N = 5 to N = 20 reflects the growing complexity of the vortex field and the onset of Lévy-like transport. However, for N = 40, α_emp drops to 1.68, indicating a transition from near-ballistic to genuinely superdiffusive transport. This non-monotonic behaviour is interpreted as follows: at low N, the vortex field is sufficiently sparse that tracers undergo quasi-ballistic advection by the dominant vortex structures; at high N, the increased vortex-vortex interactions generate a more isotropic, rapidly decorrelating velocity field that drives the tracer into a superdiffusive regime with α < 2.
